    Section 7.3 Sequences and convergence

    Note: 1 lecture
    🔗

    Subsection 7.3.1 Sequences

    The notion of a sequence in a metric space is very similar to a sequence of real numbers. The related definitions are essentially the same as those for real numbers in the sense of Chapter 2, where \(\R\) with the standard metric \(d(x,y)=\sabs{x-y}\) is replaced by an arbitrary metric space \((X,d)\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Definition 7.3.1.

    A sequence in a metric space \((X,d)\) is a function \(x \colon \N \to X\text{.}\) As before we write \(x_n\) for the \(n\)th element in the sequence, and for the whole sequence use the notation
    \begin{equation} \{ x_n \}_{n=1}^\infty . \end{equation}
    🔗
    A sequence \(\{ x_n \}_{n=1}^\infty\) is bounded if there exists a point \(p \in X\) and \(B \in \R\) such that
    \begin{equation} d(p,x_n) \leq B \qquad \text{for all } n \in \N. \end{equation}
    In other words, the sequence \(\{x_n\}_{n=1}^\infty\) is bounded whenever the set \(\{ x_n : n \in \N \}\) is bounded.
    🔗
    If \(\{ n_k \}_{k=1}^\infty\) is a sequence of natural numbers such that \(n_{k+1} > n_k\) for all \(k\text{,}\) then the sequence \(\{ x_{n_k} \}_{k=1}^\infty\) is said to be a subsequence of \(\{x_n \}_{n=1}^\infty\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Similarly, we define convergence. See Figure 7.10, for an idea of the definition.
    🔗

    Definition 7.3.2.

    A sequence \(\{ x_n \}_{n=1}^\infty\) in a metric space \((X,d)\) is said to converge to a point \(p \in X\) if for every \(\epsilon > 0\text{,}\) there exists an \(M \in \N\) such that \(d(x_n,p) < \epsilon\) for all \(n \geq M\text{.}\) The point \(p\) is said to be a limit of \(\{ x_n \}_{n=1}^\infty\text{.}\) We will prove the limit is unique, and so we write
    \begin{equation} \lim_{n\to \infty} x_n \coloneqq p . \end{equation}
    🔗
    A sequence that converges is convergent. Otherwise, the sequence is divergent.
    🔗
    🔗
    We start by proving the limit is unique. The proof is almost identical (word for word) to the proof of the same fact for sequences of real numbers, Proposition 2.1.6. Proofs of many results we know for sequences of real numbers can be adapted to the more general settings of metric spaces. We must replace \(\sabs{x-y}\) with \(d(x,y)\) in the proofs and apply the triangle inequality correctly.
    🔗

    Proposition 7.3.3.

    A convergent sequence in a metric space has a unique limit.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Suppose \(\{ x_n \}_{n=1}^\infty\) has limits \(x\) and \(y\text{.}\) Take an arbitrary \(\epsilon > 0\text{.}\) From the definition find an \(M_1\) such that for all \(n \geq M_1\text{,}\) \(d(x_n,x) < \nicefrac{\epsilon}{2}\text{.}\) Similarly, find an \(M_2\) such that for all \(n \geq M_2\text{,}\) we have \(d(x_n,y) < \nicefrac{\epsilon}{2}\text{.}\) Now take an \(n\) such that \(n \geq M_1\) and also \(n \geq M_2\text{,}\) and estimate
    \begin{equation} \begin{split} d(y,x) & \leq d(y,x_n) + d(x_n,x) \\ & < \frac{\epsilon}{2} + \frac{\epsilon}{2} = \epsilon . \end{split} \end{equation}
    As \(d(y,x) < \epsilon\) for all \(\epsilon > 0\text{,}\) we have \(d(x,y) = 0\) and so \(y=x\text{.}\) Hence, the limit (if it exists) is unique.
    🔗
    🔗
    The proofs of the following propositions are left as exercises.
    🔗

    Proposition 7.3.4.

    A convergent sequence in a metric space is bounded.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 7.3.5.

    A sequence \(\{ x_n \}_{n=1}^\infty\) in a metric space \((X,d)\) converges to \(p \in X\) if and only if there exists a sequence \(\{ a_n \}_{n=1}^\infty\) of real numbers such that
    \begin{equation} d(x_n,p) \leq a_n \quad \text{for all } n \in \N, \qquad \text{and} \qquad \lim_{n\to\infty} a_n = 0. \end{equation}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 7.3.6.

    Let \(\{ x_n \}_{n=1}^\infty\) be a sequence in a metric space \((X,d)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    1. If \(\{ x_n \}_{n=1}^\infty\) converges to \(p \in X\text{,}\) then every subsequence \(\{ x_{n_k} \}_{k=1}^\infty\) converges to \(p\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. If for some \(K \in \N\) the \(K\)-tail \(\{ x_n \}_{n=K+1}^\infty\) converges to \(p \in X\text{,}\) then \(\{ x_n \}_{n=1}^\infty\) converges to \(p\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Example 7.3.7.

    Take \(C\bigl([a,b],\R\bigr)\) to be the set of continuous functions with the metric being the uniform norm. We saw that we obtain a metric space. If we look at the definition of convergence, we notice that it is identical to uniform convergence. That is, \(\{ f_n \}_{n=1}^\infty\) converges uniformly if and only if it converges in the metric space sense.
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 7.3.8.

    It is perhaps surprising that on the set of functions \(f \colon [a,b] \to \R\) (continuous or not) there is no metric that gives pointwise convergence as the domain \([a,b]\) is uncountable. The proof of this fact is beyond the scope of this book.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 7.3.2 Convergence in euclidean space

    In the euclidean space \(\R^n\text{,}\) a sequence converges if and only if every component converges:
    🔗

    Proposition 7.3.9.

    Let \(\{ x_m \}_{m=1}^\infty\) be a sequence in \(\R^n\text{,}\) where \(x_m = \bigl(x_{m,1},x_{m,2},\ldots,x_{m,n}\bigr) \in \R^n\text{.}\) Then \(\{ x_m \}_{m=1}^\infty\) converges if and only if \(\{ x_{m,k} \}_{m=1}^\infty\) converges for every \(k=1,2,\ldots,n\text{,}\) in which case
    \begin{equation} \lim_{m\to\infty} x_m = \Bigl( \lim_{m\to\infty} x_{m,1}, \lim_{m\to\infty} x_{m,2}, \ldots, \lim_{m\to\infty} x_{m,n} \Bigr) . \end{equation}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Suppose \(\{ x_m \}_{m=1}^\infty\) converges to \(y = (y_1,y_2,\ldots,y_n) \in \R^n\text{.}\) Given \(\epsilon > 0\text{,}\) there exists an \(M\) such that for all \(m \geq M\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation} d(y,x_m) < \epsilon. \end{equation}
    Fix some \(k=1,2,\ldots,n\text{.}\) For all \(m \geq M\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation} \bigl\lvert y_k - x_{m,k} \bigr\rvert = \sqrt{{\bigl(y_k - x_{m,k} \bigr)}^2} \leq \sqrt{\sum_{\ell=1}^n {\bigl(y_\ell-x_{m,\ell}\bigr)}^2} = d(y,x_m) < \epsilon . \end{equation}
    Hence, the sequence \(\{ x_{m,k} \}_{m=1}^\infty\) converges to \(y_k\text{.}\)
    🔗
    For the other direction, suppose \(\{ x_{m,k} \}_{m=1}^\infty\) converges to \(y_k\) for every \(k=1,2,\ldots,n\text{.}\) Given \(\epsilon > 0\text{,}\) pick an \(M\) such that if \(m \geq M\text{,}\) then \(\bigl\lvert y_k-x_{m,k} \bigr\rvert < \nicefrac{\epsilon}{\sqrt{n}}\) for all \(k=1,2,\ldots,n\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation} d(y,x_m) = \sqrt{\sum_{k=1}^n {\bigl(y_k-x_{m,k}\bigr)}^2} < \sqrt{\sum_{k=1}^n {\left(\frac{\epsilon}{\sqrt{n}}\right)}^2} = \sqrt{\sum_{k=1}^n \frac{{\epsilon^2}}{n}} = \epsilon . \end{equation}
    That is, the sequence \(\{ x_m \}_{m=1}^\infty\) converges to \(y = (y_1,y_2,\ldots,y_n) \in \R^n\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 7.3.10.

    As we said, the set \(\C\) of complex numbers \(z = x+iy\) is considered as the metric space \(\R^2\text{.}\) The proposition says that the sequence \(\{ z_n \}_{n=1}^\infty = \{ x_n + iy_n \}_{n=1}^\infty\) converges to \(z = x+iy\) if and only if \(\{ x_n \}_{n=1}^\infty\) converges to \(x\) and \(\{ y_n \}_{n=1}^\infty\) converges to \(y\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 7.3.3 Convergence and topology

    The topology—the set of open sets of a space—encodes which sequences converge.
    🔗

    Proposition 7.3.11.

    Let \((X,d)\) be a metric space and \(\{x_n\}_{n=1}^\infty\) a sequence in \(X\text{.}\) Then \(\{ x_n \}_{n=1}^\infty\) converges to \(p \in X\) if and only if for every open neighborhood \(U\) of \(p\text{,}\) there exists an \(M \in \N\) such that for all \(n \geq M\text{,}\) we have \(x_n \in U\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Suppose \(\{ x_n \}_{n=1}^\infty\) converges to \(p\text{.}\) Let \(U\) be an open neighborhood of \(p\text{.}\) There exists an \(\epsilon > 0\) such that \(B(p,\epsilon) \subset U\text{.}\) As the sequence converges, find an \(M \in \N\) such that for all \(n \geq M\text{,}\) we have \(d(p,x_n) < \epsilon\text{,}\) or in other words \(x_n \in B(p,\epsilon) \subset U\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Let us prove the other direction. Given \(\epsilon > 0\text{,}\) let \(U \coloneqq B(p,\epsilon)\) be the neighborhood of \(p\text{.}\) Then there is an \(M \in \N\) such that for \(n \geq M\text{,}\) we have \(x_n \in U = B(p,\epsilon)\text{,}\) or in other words, \(d(p,x_n) < \epsilon\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    A closed set contains the limits of its convergent sequences.
    🔗

    Proposition 7.3.12.

    Let \((X,d)\) be a metric space, \(E \subset X\) a closed set, and \(\{ x_n \}_{n=1}^\infty\) a sequence in \(E\) that converges to some \(p \in X\text{.}\) Then \(p \in E\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let us prove the contrapositive. Suppose \(\{ x_n \}_{n=1}^\infty\) is a sequence in \(X\) that converges to \(p \in E^c\text{.}\) As \(E^c\) is open, Proposition 7.3.11 says that there is an \(M\) such that for all \(n \geq M\text{,}\) \(x_n \in E^c\text{.}\) So \(\{ x_n \}_{n=1}^\infty\) is not a sequence in \(E\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    To take a closure of a set \(A\text{,}\) we start with \(A\text{,}\) and we throw in points that are limits of sequences in \(A\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Proposition 7.3.13.

    Let \((X,d)\) be a metric space and \(A \subset X\text{.}\) Then \(p \in \widebar{A}\) if and only if there exists a sequence \(\{ x_n \}_{n=1}^\infty\) of elements in \(A\) such that \(\lim\limits_{n\to\infty} x_n = p\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(p \in \widebar{A}\text{.}\) For every \(n \in \N\text{,}\) Proposition 7.2.22 guarantees a point \(x_n \in B(p,\nicefrac{1}{n}) \cap A\text{.}\) As \(d(p,x_n) < \nicefrac{1}{n}\text{,}\) we have \(\lim_{n\to\infty} x_n = p\text{.}\)
    🔗
    For the other direction, see Exercise 7.3.1.
