Definition 1.1.2.

Let \(E \subset S\text{,}\) where \(S\) is an ordered set.
🔗
  1. If there exists a \(b \in S\) such that \(x \leq b\) for all \(x \in E\text{,}\) then we say \(E\) is bounded above and \(b\) is an upper bound of \(E\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
  2. If there exists a \(b \in S\) such that \(x \geq b\) for all \(x \in E\text{,}\) then we say \(E\) is bounded below and \(b\) is a lower bound of \(E\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
  3. If there exists an upper bound \(b_0\) of \(E\) such that \(b_0 \leq b\) for all upper bounds \(b\) of \(E\text{,}\) then \(b_0\) is called the least upper bound or the supremum of \(E\text{.}\) See Figure 1.1. We write
    \begin{equation} \sup\, E \coloneqq b_0 . \end{equation}
    🔗
    🔗
  4. Similarly, if there exists a lower bound \(b_0\) of \(E\) such that \(b_0 \geq b\) for all lower bounds \(b\) of \(E\text{,}\) then \(b_0\) is called the greatest lower bound or the infimum of \(E\text{.}\) We write
    \begin{equation} \inf\, E \coloneqq b_0 . \end{equation}
    🔗
    🔗
When a set \(E\) is both bounded above and bounded below, we say simply that \(E\) is bounded.
