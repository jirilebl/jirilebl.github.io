Example 3.2.11.

For an extreme example, consider the Dirichlet function
 1 
Named after the German mathematician Johann Peter Gustav Lejeune Dirichlet (1805–1859).
.
\begin{equation} f(x) \coloneqq \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } x \text{ is rational,} \\ 0 & \text{if } x \text{ is irrational.} \end{cases} \end{equation}
The function \(f\) is discontinuous at all \(c \in \R\text{.}\)
🔗
Proof: If \(c\) is rational, take a sequence \(\{ x_n \}_{n=1}^\infty\) of irrational numbers in \((0,1)\) such that \(\lim_{n\to\infty} x_n = c\) (why can we?). Then \(f(x_n) = 0\) and so \(\lim_{n\to\infty} f(x_n) = 0\text{,}\) but \(f(c) = 1\text{.}\) If \(c\) is irrational, take a sequence \(\{ x_n \}_{n=1}^\infty\) of rational numbers in \((0,1)\) that converges to \(c\) (why can we?). Then \(\lim_{n\to\infty} f(x_n) = 1\text{,}\) but \(f(c) = 0\text{.}\)
