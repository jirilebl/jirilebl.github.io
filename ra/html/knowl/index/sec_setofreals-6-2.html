By Exercise 1.1.2, a finite set of numbers always has a supremum and an infimum and they are both contained in the set itself. In this case, we usually do not use the words supremum or infimum. When a set \(A\) of real numbers is bounded above and \(\sup\, A \in A\text{,}\) we can use the word maximum and the notation \(\max\, A\) to denote the supremum. Similarly for infimum: When \(A\) is bounded below and \(\inf\, A \in A\text{,}\) we can use the word minimum and the notation \(\min\, A\text{.}\) For example,
\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} & \max \{ 1,2.4,\pi,100 \} = 100 , \\ & \min \{ 1,2.4,\pi,100 \} = 1 . \end{aligned} \end{equation}
While writing \(\sup\) and \(\inf\) may be technically correct in this situation, \(\max\) and \(\min\) are generally used to emphasize that the supremum or infimum is in the set itself, especially when the set is finite.
