Proposition 11.7.11.

Suppose \(\sA\) is an algebra of complex-valued functions on a set \(X\) that separates points and vanishes at no point. Suppose \(x,y\) are distinct points of \(X\text{,}\) and \(c,d \in \C\text{.}\) Then there is an \(f \in \sA\) such that
\begin{equation} f(x) = c, \qquad f(y) = d . \end{equation}
If \(\sA\) is a real algebra, the conclusion holds for \(c,d \in \R\text{.}\)
