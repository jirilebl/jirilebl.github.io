Example 4.2.12.

Let \(f \colon \R \to \R\) be the function defined by
\begin{equation} f(x) \coloneqq \begin{cases} {\bigl( x \sin(\nicefrac{1}{x}) \bigr)}^2 & \text{if } x \neq 0, \\ 0 & \text{if } x = 0. \end{cases} \end{equation}
We claim that \(f\) is differentiable everywhere, but \(f' \colon \R \to \R\) is not continuous at the origin. Furthermore, \(f\) has a minimum at \(0\text{,}\) but the derivative changes sign infinitely often near the origin. See Figure 4.7.
🔗
Proof: It is immediate from the definition that \(f\) has an absolute minimum at \(0\text{;}\) we know \(f(x) \geq 0\) for all \(x\) and \(f(0) = 0\text{.}\)
🔗
For \(x \neq 0\text{,}\) \(f\) is differentiable and the derivative is \(2 \sin (\nicefrac{1}{x}) \bigl( x \sin (\nicefrac{1}{x}) - \cos(\nicefrac{1}{x}) \bigr)\text{.}\) As an exercise, show that for \(x_n = \frac{4}{(8n+1)\pi}\text{,}\) we have \(\lim_{n\to\infty} f'(x_n) = -1\text{,}\) and for \(y_n = \frac{4}{(8n+3)\pi}\text{,}\) we have \(\lim_{n\to\infty} f'(y_n) = 1\text{.}\) So \(f'\) cannot be continuous at \(0\) no matter what \(f'(0)\) is.
🔗
Let us show that \(f\) is differentiable at \(0\) and \(f'(0)=0\text{.}\) For \(x \neq 0\text{,}\)
\begin{equation} \abs{\frac{f(x)-f(0)}{x-0} - 0} = \abs{\frac{x^2 \sin^2(\nicefrac{1}{x})}{x}} = \babs{x \sin^2(\nicefrac{1}{x})} \leq \sabs{x} . \end{equation}
And, of course, as \(x\) tends to zero, \(\sabs{x}\) tends to zero, and hence \(\abs{\frac{f(x)-f(0)}{x-0} - 0}\) goes to zero. Therefore, \(f\) is differentiable at 0 and the derivative at 0 is 0. A key point in the calculation above is that \(\babs{f(x)} \leq x^2\text{,}\) see also Exercises 4.1.11 and 4.1.12.
