The trick is to rewrite the left-hand side of (1.3) as a derivative of a product of \(y\) with another function. To this end, we wish to find a function \(r(x)\) such that
\begin{equation} r(x) y' + r(x) p(x) y = \frac{d}{dx}\Bigl[ r(x) y \Bigr] . \end{equation}
This is the left-hand side of (1.3) multiplied by \(r(x)\text{.}\) If we multiply (1.3) by \(r(x)\text{,}\) we obtain
\begin{equation} \frac{d}{dx}\Bigl[ r(x) y \Bigr] = r(x)f(x) . \end{equation}
We can now integrate both sides, which we can do as the right-hand side does not depend on \(y\) and the left-hand side is written as a derivative of a function. After the integration, we solve for \(y\) by dividing by \(r(x)\text{.}\) The function \(r(x)\) is called the integrating factor and the method is called the integrating factor method.
