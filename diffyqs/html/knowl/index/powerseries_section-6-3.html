Example 7.1.5.

Let us show that the exponential \(y=e^x\) solves \(y'=y\text{.}\) Suppose we did not know that. Write
\begin{equation} y = e^x = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{1}{k!} x^k . \end{equation}
Differentiate \(y\text{,}\)
\begin{equation} y' = \sum_{k=1}^\infty k \frac{1}{k!} x^{k-1} = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{1}{(k-1)!} x^{k-1} . \end{equation}
We reindex the series by simply replacing \(k\) with \(k+1\text{.}\) The series does not change, what changes is simply how we write it. After reindexing the series starts at \(k=0\) again.
\begin{equation} \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{1}{(k-1)!} x^{k-1} = \sum_{k+1=1}^\infty \frac{1}{\bigl((k+1)-1\bigr)!} x^{(k+1)-1} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{1}{k!} x^k . \end{equation}
That was precisely the power series for \(e^x\) we started with, so we showed that \(\frac{d}{dx} [ e^x ] = e^x\text{.}\)
