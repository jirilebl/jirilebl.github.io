Our next example behaves like a mass and spring system only approximately. Suppose a mass of \(m\) kilograms hangs on a pendulum of length \(L\) meters. We seek an equation for the angle \(\theta(t)\) (in radians). Let \(g\) be the acceleration due to gravity in meters per second squared. Let us derive an equation for \(\theta\) using Newton’s second law: Force equals mass times acceleration. The acceleration is \(L \theta''\) and mass is \(m\text{.}\) So \(mL\theta''\) has to be equal to the tangential component of the force given by the gravity, which is \(m g \sin \theta\) in the opposite direction. So \(mL\theta'' = -mg \sin \theta\text{.}\) The \(m\) curiously cancels from the equation and we end up with the equation
\begin{equation} \theta'' + \frac{g}{L} \sin \theta = 0 . \end{equation}
