We define some operations on matrices. We want \(1 \times 1\) matrices to really act like numbers, so our operations have to be compatible with this viewpoint. First, we can multiply a matrix by a scalar (a number). We simply multiply each entry in the matrix by the scalar. For example,
\begin{equation} 2 \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 & 6 \\ 8 & 10 & 12 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation}
Matrix addition is also simple. We add matrices element by element. For example,
\begin{equation} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & -1 \\ 0 & 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 & 2 \\ 4 & 7 & 10 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation}
If the sizes do not match, then addition is not defined. If we denote by 0 the matrix with all zero entries, by \(c\text{,}\) \(d\) scalars, and by \(A\text{,}\) \(B\text{,}\) \(C\) matrices, we have the following familiar rules:
\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} A + 0 & = A = 0 + A , \\ A + B & = B + A , \\ (A + B) + C & = A + (B + C) , \\ c(A+B) & = cA+cB, \\ (c+d)A & = cA + dA. \end{aligned} \end{equation}
