Example 6.1.6.

Take \(F(s) = \frac{s^2+s+1}{s^3+s}\text{.}\) Find the inverse Laplace transform.

First we use the method of partial fractions to write \(F\) in a form where we can use TableÂ 6.1. We factor the denominator as \(s(s^2+1)\) and write
\begin{equation} \frac{s^2+s+1}{s^3+s} = \frac{A}{s} + \frac{Bs+C}{s^2+1} . \end{equation}
Putting the right-hand side over a common denominator and equating the numerators, we get \(A(s^2+1) + s(Bs+C) = s^2+s+1\text{.}\) Expanding and equating coefficients, we obtain \(A+B = 1\text{,}\) \(C=1\text{,}\) \(A=1\text{,}\) and thus \(B=0\text{.}\) In other words,
\begin{equation} F(s) = \frac{s^2+s+1}{s^3+s} = \frac{1}{s} + \frac{1}{s^2+1} . \end{equation}
By linearity of the inverse Laplace transform, we get
\begin{equation} {\mathcal{L}}^{-1} \left\{ \frac{s^2+s+1}{s^3+s} \right\} = {\mathcal{L}}^{-1} \left\{ \frac{1}{s} \right\} + {\mathcal{L}}^{-1} \left\{ \frac{1}{s^2+1} \right\} = 1 + \sin t . \end{equation}
