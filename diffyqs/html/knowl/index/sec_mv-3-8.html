Here is an example for electrical engineers. Consider the pictured RLC circuit. There is a resistor with a resistance of \(R\) ohms, an inductor with an inductance of \(L\) henries, and a capacitor with a capacitance of \(C\) farads. There is also an electric source (such as a battery) with voltage of \(E(t)\) volts at time \(t\) (measured in seconds). Let \(Q(t)\) be the charge (in coulombs) on the capacitor and \(I(t)\) be the current (in amperes) in the circuit. The relation between the two is \(Q' = I\text{.}\) By elementary principles, we find \(L I' + RI + \nicefrac{Q}{C} = E\text{.}\) We differentiate to get
\begin{equation} L I''(t) + R I'(t) + \frac{1}{C} I(t) = E'(t) . \end{equation}
This is a nonhomogeneous second-order constant-coefficient linear equation. As \(L, R\text{,}\) and \(C\) are all positive, this circuit behaves just like the mass and spring system. Position of the mass is replaced by current. Mass is replaced by inductance, damping is replaced by resistance, and the spring constant is replaced by one over the capacitance.
