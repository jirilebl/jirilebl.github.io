One thing to take away from all this is how a guitar sounds. Notice that the (angular) frequencies that come up in the solution are \(n \frac{\pi a}{L}\text{.}\) That is, there is a certain base fundamental frequency \(\frac{\pi a}{L}\text{,}\) and then we also get all the multiples of this frequency, which in music are called the overtones. Which overtones appear and with what amplitude is what musicians call the timbre of the note. Mathematicians usually call this the spectrum. Because all the frequencies are multiples of one frequency (the fundamental), we get a nice pleasing sound.
